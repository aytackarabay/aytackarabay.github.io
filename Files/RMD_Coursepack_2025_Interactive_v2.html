Course Pack 4 Part 1

Module Structure and Learning Outcomes

On completion of this module, you should be able to achieve the following learning outcomes:

- Demonstrate a breadth of knowledge about the concepts and scope of relational approaches in psychological research.

- Demonstrate a breadth of knowledge in choosing appropriate methods of analysis in different research contexts.

- Describe and justify the design and validation of their own questionnaire using appropriate written reporting conventions.

- Carry out and interpret various statistical relational analyses using Jamovi and R Studio.

- Conduct an appropriately designed research study drawing on relational approaches and describe and justify their own research in the presence of others using standard oral reporting conventions.

Assessment on Research Methods D

Students who have completed a degree which is accredited by the British Psychological Society (BPS) are expected to be able to have research skills. Research Methods D continues your training and education in quantitative research methods and analyses, with a focus on questionnaire design, validation, application and evaluation. You will also need to draw on content from previous research methods modules.

Questionnaires are not only important methodological tool for Psychologists and Neuroscientists, they are also useful in many other settings including business, retail, industry, healthcare, social care, fashion, entertainment, education, sales, politics, sociology, consumer research, transport etc. Questionnaires can also be in a wide variety of formats including print, online via a survey platform, verbal, in-person, telephone, text, in social media apps etc. This module will teach you all of the necessary steps to design, plot, refine, validate, apply and evaluate an online questionnaire using Microsoft Forms, and you will be assessed in the following ways:

• Your ability to design, pilot, refine and validate a new questionnaire will be assessed in the group poster presentation. For this assessment, you will need to draw on the lectures, workshop instructions, your reading and sections 1-3 of this course pack (item vetting, item analysis and factor analysis).

• Your ability to apply and further evaluate this new questionnaire will then be assessed in the individual 2000-word research report. For this assessment, you will need to draw on the lectures, workshop instructions, reading around the topic and all sections of this course pack (e.g. selecting an appropriate analysis to test your relational hypotheses).

It is also important to note that the Research Methods course packs will also be useful for final year projects.

Week 1: Item Wording/Vetting and Partial Correlation

Learning Objectives

When you have completed this workshop, you should be able to:

1. Understand the need for careful wording in questionnaire design, distinguishing between different question features that must be considered.
2. Distinguish between well-worded and poorly-worded items.
3. Create a scatter plot in Jamovi to observe and describe relationships between variables.
4. Create a scatter plot in Jamovi to observe and describe the relationship between two variables when data are grouped by a third variable.
5. Conduct bivariate correlation between pairs of variables in Jamovi, interpret the output and write-up the findings.
6. Conduct partial correlations between two variables in Jamovi, interpret the output and write-up the findings.
7. Examine correlational data using scatter plots to make decisions about how to test the relationship between variables.

Item Wording

Here is a list of things to bear in mind when writing questionnaire items. Read through and make sure you understand each potential limitation and how to avoid them

Things to bear in mind Explanation
Use simple language Avoid jargon, complicated language or technical terms, keep it simple so anyone can understand.
Keep questions short Long questions confuse or bore participants. Keep questions as short as possible.
Avoid double barrelled questions Asking two things in one question confuses participants and produces meaningless data. If you want to ask two things, split into two questions.
Avoid leading questions We want to know what our participants think and feel, not our own views reflected back. Don’t imply one answer is ‘right’ or ‘better’.
Avoid double negatives Double negatives are confusing and lead to meaningless data. Consider wording carefully.
Is the respondent likely to know the answer? If the question is knowledge-based, participants should be able to answer. Consider what your participants can reasonably be expected to know.
Are the meanings of words clear? Some words have more than one meaning. For example, a bat can mean a mammal, a cricket bat, or to hit. Ensure your meaning is clear.
Avoid ‘prestige bias’ The perceived prestige of a person, brand, job etc. can influence responses. Avoid implying superiority to obtain genuine responses.
Avoid ‘conformity bias’ and social desirability We often conform to what we think is common or desirable, which may not reflect actual behaviour. Emphasise the importance of honest responses.
Avoid ambiguity Questions can often be interpreted in several ways. Make sure your question is clear and unambiguous.
Is the context clear? Words and questions can have different meanings in different contexts. Be explicit about which context you mean.
Avoid questions that create opinions Questionnaires should explore what participants think or feel, not influence or change their opinions.
Avoid causing offence Offending participants can result in withdrawal and complaints. Avoid discriminatory language and respect protected characteristics.

Item Vetting Task

Look at the list of questions/items below that were designed to explore life as a student. A participant would respond with their level of agreement to the items below using a 7-point Likert scale ranging from ‘strongly agree’ to ‘strongly disagree’. Read through the items and see if you think any should be removed or re-written. Make a list of alterations (15 min)

1. I carefully considered other options before choosing to come to university.
2. By working diligently, students earn the minimal funding they receive.
3. It is not the case that students are not as well off as they used to be.
4. Like successful graduates, I believe students should pay for their own education.
5. Radical politicians should be allowed to cut student incomes to poverty levels.
6. University will provide me with either a wider view of life or a new path in life’s big journey.
7. One hundred pounds is a good amount.
8. University life is providing me with new opportunities.
9. My degree is the most important area of my life.
10. Indolent student politicians are responsible for decimating maintenance grants.
11. I balance my studying and socialising well.
12. I make an important contribution to the University community.
13. After getting my degree I will have a better chance in the British or international job market.
14. It is an honest person that believes all education should be free
15. Free education is an important cornerstone to our society.
16. I believe that students should receive more financial support.
17. Questionnaires about student opinions are a waste of time.
18. When I started at University I believed I would ultimately improve my quality of life by undertaking further education.

If you are not sure about any of these you can ask staff in your workshop, but ultimately you will need to make the decision because you need to be able to defend the methodological and statistical choices you make.

Creating a scatter plot with Jamovi

Before correlating two variables, it is always a good idea to produce a scatter plot of the relationship between two variables. Scatter plots help you to decide which method for calculating a correlation coefficient to use. Scatter plots also tell you if you should perform a correlation analysis at all.
Each point on a scatter plot represents an individual in the sample. The point’s position on the X and Y axes corresponds to that individual’s scores on the two variables. 1. Select analysis -> exploration -> scatterplot

2. You will see your variables, in this screen (see below). Move the “engagement” and “acadsuccess” variables to the X & Y axis. Either by dragging the variable names to the X/Y axis box, or selecting the variable and click on the arrows next to the box.

3. Once you have populated both X and Y axis labels, the Scatterplot will automatically appear.

Answer Section
Based on the scatter plot answer these key questions about the data.
1. Is the relationship approximately linear?
2. Is the relationship likely to be strong?
3. Is the relationship likely to be positive or negative?
4. Are there any obvious outliers?
